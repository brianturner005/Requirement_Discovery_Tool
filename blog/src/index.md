## I. The Hook

Something shifted in American music around 1991. Most people didn't notice. The ones who did mostly shrugged. That's how these things work.

I grew up in South Carolina. We made fun of people who listened to country music — not maliciously, just the way kids signal who they are by signaling who they aren't. Country was a marker. It told you something about the person: older, rural, the guy with the gun rack who didn't have a lot of patience for questions. We weren't being fair. But we weren't entirely wrong either.

Here's what I'll give country music before I bury it: the old stuff was real. Hank Williams. Merle Haggard. Early Waylon and Willie. That music had actual grit — working-class heartbreak, outlaw defiance, the kind of honesty that doesn't pretty itself up for anybody. I'm not talking about that music. That music isn't the problem.

The problem is what came after. What Garth Brooks became in 1991: the best-selling musical artist in America. Not best-selling country artist. Best-selling *everything*. Michael Jackson. Madonna. U2. All of them outpaced by a man in a hat with a polished stadium show and an identity pre-packaged for mass consumption. And millions of people bought it — not just the album, but the whole package. Who it said they were. What it said they stood for. And, eventually, who it said wasn't them.

They went along with it. That's what this essay is about. Not the music. The going-along. The slow, comfortable slide into letting an industry hand you your identity — and then watching that same industry decide, year by year, what that identity was *against*.

We're living in the results. Let me show you how we got here.

The correlation is not subtle once you look for it. Consider two parallel tracks running through the last four decades of American life:

These are not coincidences. They are the visible symptoms of a single underlying process.

## III. The Identity Machine

Every genre of music creates an identity. Hip-hop says something about who you are. So does classical, or jazz, or punk. But country music in its post-1990 mainstream incarnation did something different and more politically potent: it created an identity that was explicitly *national* while being implicitly *exclusive*.

To like country music — really like it, perform liking it, put the sticker on your truck — was to make a claim. The claim was: *I am the real America.* And the unspoken corollary, just as important: *you are not.*

This is not an accident. It is the product of deliberate branding decisions made by the Nashville industry, radio programmers, and later political consultants who understood exactly what they had. Country music became the cultural marker of a coalition — rural, white, Christian, Southern (and increasingly, ex-urban and Midwestern) — that felt itself under siege by demographic change, coastal condescension, and a cultural mainstream that had, in their view, written them out of the story of modern America.

The music didn't cause this feeling. But it codified it. It gave it a soundtrack, a wardrobe, a vocabulary, and crucially, a sense of collective identity that could be mobilized.

## IV. The Dixie Chicks Incident

No single event illustrates the political function of country music more clearly than what happened to the Dixie Chicks in March 2003.

At a concert in London, lead singer Natalie Maines said nine words: *"We're ashamed that the President of the United States is from Texas."*

What followed was not a controversy. It was a demonstration of power.

Country radio stations — most of them owned by Clear Channel — coordinated a near-total ban of the Dixie Chicks from their playlists within days. Organized "CD smashing" events were held across the South. Death threats were received. The most commercially successful female group in country history was effectively disappeared from its own genre, not for a crime, not for a scandal, but for a political opinion expressed in another country, on the eve of an unpopular war.

What the Dixie Chicks incident revealed was that country music's gatekeepers — the radio conglomerates, the Nashville establishment, the fan base as organized political force — had the will and the infrastructure to enforce political conformity. It was a dress rehearsal. A proof of concept.

The lesson absorbed by every country artist who came after: know your lane. Know your politics. Or be erased.

## V. Corporate Radio and the Monoculture

You cannot understand country music's political function without understanding what the 1996 Telecommunications Act did to American radio.

Before 1996, federal rules limited a single company to owning no more than 40 radio stations nationally. After 1996, there were effectively no limits. Clear Channel — later renamed iHeartMedia — went from owning 40 stations to owning over 1,200. A single corporation was now programming music taste for most of the country.

The effect on country music was dramatic and deliberate. Country radio had always been popular; now it became a national delivery system. And because iHeart's country stations all ran from centralized programming lists, the same 30 songs played in Memphis, Denver, Charlotte, and Phoenix. Regional variation — the thing that had made American music so vital for a century — was systematically eliminated.

What replaced it was a product: a polished, focus-grouped, demographically targeted version of country music designed to feel authentic while being anything but. The music said: trucks, tailgates, small towns, the flag, God, and people who work with their hands. It said: *we are all the same.* And in saying so, it made a quiet political argument that the culturally diverse, cosmopolitan, multiracial, multi-faith reality of actual America was somehow less real — less legitimate — than this constructed vision of it.

## VI. Nostalgia as a Weapon

Every country song about "the way things used to be" is making a political argument, whether or not the songwriter intends it.

Nostalgia is not inherently political. But weaponized nostalgia — nostalgia deployed to make people feel that something valuable has been stolen from them, and to identify who stole it — is one of the most effective tools in the authoritarian playbook. It produces grievance. It produces longing. And most importantly, it produces a willingness to trade complex, slow, frustrating democratic processes for a simpler story: *someone took what was ours, and we want it back.*

Country music spent thirty years perfecting this emotional register. Small towns, front porches, Friday nights at the drive-in, a time when men were men and neighbors were neighbors and you knew who you were. It is beautiful music, some of it. It is also a sustained argument that the present is a betrayal of the past, and that the people responsible for that betrayal are identifiable and punishable.

"Make America Great Again" is a country song. It has all the elements: the appeal to lost golden age, the implicit enemy, the promise of restoration, the rural/small-town setting, the masculine simplicity. The only thing it's missing is a steel guitar.

## VII. The Proof

The geographic overlap between the highest concentration of country music listeners and the highest concentration of support for democratic backsliding is not subtle. Red-state maps and country radio ratings maps are essentially the same document.

But the more interesting data point is the *change over time.* In the early 1990s, country music audiences were concentrated in the South and parts of the rural Midwest. By 2016, country music was the dominant radio format across most of the non-coastal United States. And in that same period, the political geography of America had shifted in precisely the same direction — away from the coasts, away from cities, toward a kind of rural white identity politics that had previously been a regional quirk and was now a national force.

The music didn't just reflect this shift. It helped cause it. By creating a shared cultural identity — by giving disparate rural and suburban white communities the same songs, the same imagery, the same sense of embattled authenticity — country music built the social infrastructure that political movements require.

You cannot organize people who have no sense of common identity. Country music gave that identity to tens of millions of Americans who might otherwise have remained a demographic and not a movement.

## VIII. The Verdict

None of this means country music is evil. None of this means that people who love country music are enemies of democracy. Music is not a political party, and people are not their playlists.

But ideas have delivery mechanisms. And for thirty years, one of the most powerful delivery mechanisms in American culture has been systematically transmitting a set of ideas — about who is real, who belongs, who is legitimate, and who is not — that are fundamentally incompatible with the pluralist, multi-everything democracy that the American experiment has always, imperfectly and incompletely, aspired to be.

The rise of country music to cultural dominance is the story of a coalition that felt itself losing the culture and decided, consciously or not, to use every available lever — radio consolidation, industry gatekeeping, political alignment — to claim the culture back. And in doing so, it modeled and normalized exactly the behaviors that now define our political moment: the purity tests, the organized cancellations, the conflation of identity with loyalty, the contempt for complexity.

We are living in the America that country music built. And we need to understand how we got here if we have any hope of imagining our way to somewhere else.
